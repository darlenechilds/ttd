Use of CFCs and SF₆ in the Ocean
1. Tracers for Ocean Circulation and Ventilation
CFCs and SF₆ are widely used as transient tracers in the study of ocean circulation and water mass ventilation. These compounds are man-made gases that were introduced into the atmosphere in the mid-20th century and have known atmospheric histories.

Why They Work as Tracers:
Inert in seawater: They do not react chemically or biologically, making them stable over time.

Known atmospheric histories: The time-varying concentrations of CFCs and SF₆ in the atmosphere are well-documented.

Soluble in seawater: They dissolve in surface waters and are transported with water masses into the ocean interior.

2. Estimating Water Mass Ages (Transit Time Distributions)
By measuring the concentration of CFCs and SF₆ in a water sample and comparing it to their known atmospheric histories, scientists can estimate:

When the water was last in contact with the atmosphere (i.e., the “apparent age” of the water).

The rate of deep ocean ventilation, which is crucial for understanding carbon uptake and heat transport.

CFC-11, CFC-12, and CFC-113 are the most commonly used variants.
They are particularly useful for waters ventilated since the 1950s–1970s.

Their usage has declined due to international bans (e.g., the Montreal Protocol), limiting their usefulness in younger waters.

SF₆ is increasingly used because:
It has continued atmospheric growth.

It is useful in tracing newly ventilated water masses and has applications in shorter-term studies.

3. Use in Deliberate Tracer Release Experiments
SF₆ is also used in controlled experiments:

Researchers inject a known quantity of SF₆ into the ocean and track its dispersion over time.

This helps study mixing processes, dispersion rates, and ocean turbulence.
