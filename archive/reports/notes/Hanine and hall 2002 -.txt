Haine & Hall (2002) provided a rigorous mathematical theory for interpreting ocean tracers in terms of distinct water‑mass sources and their age distributions. By using the boundary propagator—essentially a superposition kernel—they enabled tracer data to reveal the full age spectrum and origin composition of ocean waters, not just average ages. This framework underpins modern tracer and TTD analyses, helping researchers reconstruct water formation history and mixing pathways more accurately.

Hall & Haine (2002) deliver a rigorous theoretical advance by showing that the transient signal of the idealized age tracer in ocean models can be mathematically interpreted as the actual age distribution of water parcels.
This bridges the gap between classic age tracers and full TTD theory, and provides a powerful tool for diagnosing ocean ventilation in both models and observations.

The foundational work of Haine and Hall (2001, 2002) provided a theoretical framework that links tracer-based diagnostics to water mass age and origin through the use of boundary propagators and idealized age tracers. Their ideas have been widely expanded by subsequent researchers. Waugh, Hall, and colleagues applied the transit time distribution (TTD) approach using tracer pairs such as CFC-12 and SF₆ to quantify ventilation and constrain the relative influence of advection versus diffusion. Observational oceanographers like Monika Rhein, William Smethie, and John Smith further extended the method by fitting TTDs to measured tracer concentrations in the North Atlantic and Arctic, often using multi-tracer combinations. In parallel, researchers like Holzer, Primeau, DeVries, and Khatiwala embedded TTD theory into inverse models and Earth system diagnostics, enabling global reconstructions of anthropogenic CO₂, oxygen, and nutrient cycling. These advancements, grounded in Haine and Hall’s formalism, have become integral to modern oceanography, helping to evaluate models, interpret global datasets (e.g., GLODAP, GEOTRACES), and quantify the ocean’s role in climate through ventilation age, mixing, and carbon storage.

Using two transient tracers in TTD analysis (like CFC-12 and SF₆) enables the simultaneous estimation of both the mean age and mixing intensity of a water mass. It improves accuracy, reduces ambiguity, and provides deeper insights into the mechanisms of ocean ventilation and tracer transport.
